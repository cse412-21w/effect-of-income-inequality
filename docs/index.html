The balance of income inequality is oft debated. In particular, many studies make the argument that the existence of income inequality is necessary and is simply a part of the nature of a free-market economy that acts as a part of the development of society. For example, by working hard, taking risks, and investing, any individual in a free-market economy may climb to the highest economic income status and achieve wealth. However, critics strongly highlight that such a wealth gap creates severely unfair environments for the lower-income groups of society and can lead to social instability. Effects of economic inequality can lead to effects such as the inhibition of education achievement and even long-term mental health issues for lower-income groups. Both sides of the argument regarding income inequality make strong points. The existence of income inequality is undeniable and will continue to exist for as long as societies exist as a fuel for social development. However, what is the ultimate balance between income inequality and maintaining a healthy society? How income inequality is measured?

One of the most common and effective ways to measure the inequality of a country is by using the Gini index, or Gini coefficient. It measures the distribution of income across a population. The index ranges from 0 to 1, and 1 represents that society is perfectly unequal. Furthermore, most of the data, especially the U.S., mainly comes from the IRS income data and the Census survey. Therefore, one should keep in mind that the data may not reflect the actual picture of the current income gap because there are more ways for the rich to move their wealth here and there to avoid paying higher taxes.

Historical Trend of Income Gap

As many have argued, income inequality is a natural byproduct of societal growth. Proponents of income inequality often cite that societies naturally reward those who work hard and take opportunities, thus a distribution of incomes is inevitable. Statistically, this distribution of income across the world in a multitude of countries and societies does demonstrate income inequality and can be seen in the visuals below. In particular, the United States of America serves as an interesting case study for income inequality. Despite being the wealthiest country in the world and often being viewed as the face of capitalist economies, the USA experiences severe income inequality.

According to a study Link. performed by the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities (CBPP) on the United States of America Internal Revenue Service data, the income gap in the USA experienced little to no change between the late 1940’s and the early 1970’s. All classes of income distribution experienced consistent, strong growth. However, as economic growth rapidly slowed in the early 1970’s, the income gap began to widen. Wealth slowly became more concentrated at the upper levels of income distribution and the highest income group in the United States continued its upper trajectory in growth over time. However, the middle and lower class income groups experienced stagnation and eventually steady decline over time. As the economy slowed, smaller businesses and lower income workers lacked the financial powers and savings to withstand the economic impacts.

Potential Social Problems

Although there is no direct evidence linking income inequality with mental health disorders, namely depression, many studies have found a strong correlation between the two factors. One example of this can be seen in India, where a survey was conducted on Indian citizens in 12 different states. The randomly selected participants were divided into quintiles based on income, from the lowest fifth to the highest fifth earners. On a 95% confidence interval, it was found that the current weighted prevalence of participants in the lowest quintile of income was a mere 2.68%. Current weighted prevalence is described as a subject that has suffered from depression within 2 weeks of the study surveying. Further, a static visual of these statistics indicates a strong correlation between depression disorder rates and income status. As income increases, notable decreases in depression disorder rates are seen, both current and in terms of lifetime.

Another visual can illuminate a reason for this trend. Often, higher GDP or wealthier nations around the globe spend have increased spending on mental health care infrastructure, thus increasing the population’s access to mental health workers. As an individual’s income increases, their ability to spend extra money on mental health care also increases, which could explain lower rates of mental health issues.

A further explanation for this trend could also be higher income families often experience the effects of increased educational quality. Access to such experiences allow for wealthier children to inherently acquire higher paying jobs. As such, these children will have increased income to spend on mental health care as opposed to children of lower income status. Additionally, in the United States, since public school districts are funded by property tax, economic segregation of school districts will increase as income gaps widen between neighborhoods. Ultimately, many factors play into income inequality, and these effects accumulate into inequalities in other aspects of life.
